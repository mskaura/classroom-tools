🤴 Fur Trade Negotiator

Step into the world of New France in the 1700s. Choose your perspective and make three rounds of trade decisions — then discover how your choices compare to history.

Choose your perspective to begin:

🤶
Indigenous Trader
You are an Algonquin trading leader navigating European contact, protecting your community and traditional territory.
⚓️
French/English Fur Trader
You are a coureur de bois trying to build a profitable fur trade network in New France under pressure from your company.
Round 1 of 3
Round 1: The First Meeting
It is 1715 at a trading post near the St. Lawrence River. French traders arrive with metal tools, blankets, and muskets. They want beaver pelts. You are an Algonquin trading leader. What do you offer?
Round 2 of 3
Round 2: The Dependency Trap
Three years later, your community has become accustomed to European metal tools and blankets. The French now demand exclusivity — you must trade only with them. A British trader has arrived offering better prices. What do you do?
Round 3 of 3
Round 3: The Long-Term Consequence
It is 1750. The beaver population has been dramatically reduced due to over-trapping across the region. Disease has struck your community. The French are pushing your people further from your traditional territory. You must make a strategic decision.
Round 1 of 3
Round 1: Establishing Trade
It is 1715. You are a French coureur de bois (fur trader) trying to establish relations with the Algonquin people. Your company needs beaver pelts to supply European hat markets. What is your approach?
Round 2 of 3
Round 2: Supply Chain Pressure
Your company in Paris demands more pelts. The beaver population near settlements is declining. First Nations are pushing for better terms. Meanwhile, the British are offering guns more freely. What do you do?
Round 3 of 3
Round 3: The Legacy Question
It is 1750. Competition with the British is intensifying. New France's survival depends on First Nations alliances. How do you approach the relationship for the future?

Grade 7 — History
New France and British North America: New France and the Fur Trade 1713–1850
A2.3 — The Fur Trade Economy and Indigenous Peoples
Describe the economic and social effects of the fur trade on Indigenous peoples and European colonists, and explain how the trade shaped power relationships in New France.
A1.3 — Multiple Perspectives in History
Demonstrate the ability to analyse historical events from multiple perspectives, including those of Indigenous peoples and European colonists, and explain how perspective influences historical interpretation.
How this tool addresses it: Students play as either an Indigenous trader or a French coureur de bois across three rounds of negotiation decisions. Immediate consequences mirror documented historical patterns (dependency cycles, alliance strategies, ecological collapse). Playing both perspectives builds the historical empathy and multi-perspective analysis skills central to Ontario's historical thinking curriculum.
