💖 Residential Schools

A guided timeline through the history of the residential school system in Canada — presented with care, respect, and truth. Every child matters.

🍑 Every Child Matters — Wearing orange honours the children who were taken and those who never came home 🍑
1870s–1880s
The System Begins
The Canadian government, influenced by a policy of 'aggressive assimilation,' began forcibly removing Indigenous children from their families and communities. The Indian Act (1876) gave the government sweeping control over Indigenous peoples' lives. By 1883, the first government-funded, church-run residential schools opened in Western Canada. The goal, stated plainly by the first Deputy Superintendent of Indian Affairs: 'kill the Indian in the child.'
They took us away from everything we knew. We were punished for speaking our language. I didn't understand why they hated who I were. — Survivor testimony, TRC Final Report

1890s–1910s
Expansion Across Canada
The residential school system expanded rapidly, with schools in every province and territory except PEI and Newfoundland. Children as young as 4 were taken — sometimes by police, sometimes by officials who threatened parents with jail. Conditions were often poor: overcrowding, inadequate food, and forced labour were common. Many children died of disease, particularly tuberculosis.
I was hungry all the time. They made us work in the fields and clean the school. We were free labour. Some of my friends never came home. — Survivor testimony, TRC Final Report

1920s–1940s
A System Under Strain
Dr. Peter Henderson Bryce, the government's chief medical inspector, reported in 1907 that death rates at residential schools were catastrophically high — up to 69% in some schools. His report was suppressed. The government made attendance mandatory in 1920. Abuse — physical, emotional, and sexual — was widespread and systemic. Children who ran away were returned by police.
If you ran away they'd bring you back and strap you in front of everyone. I learned to feel nothing. That was the only way to survive. — Survivor testimony, TRC Final Report

1950s–1960s
The Sixties Scoop
Even as some residential schools closed, a new wave of removal began: Indigenous children were taken from their homes and placed with non-Indigenous families — the 'Sixties Scoop.' The federal government transferred control of some schools to provinces, but the harm continued. The last federally run residential school would not close until 1997.
I was adopted by a white family who didn't understand who I were. I lost my identity twice — once from the residential school my parents attended, and again when I was scooped. — Survivor testimony, TRC Final Report

1970s–1996
Resistance and the Last Schools
Indigenous peoples organized and resisted. In 1973, the National Indian Brotherhood called for Indigenous control over Indigenous education. Schools slowly closed under public pressure and political change. The last federally run residential school — Gordon's Indian Residential School in Saskatchewan — closed in 1996. Over 150,000 children had attended these schools. At least 4,000 are confirmed dead; the true number is likely far higher.
When I finally left, I had no idea how to be a mother. They took that from me too. My children suffered for what was done to me. — Survivor testimony, TRC Final Report

💖 Truth and Reconciliation

The Truth and Reconciliation Commission of Canada (2008–2015) heard testimony from thousands of survivors. Its 94 Calls to Action call on all Canadians — governments, churches, schools, and individuals — to take concrete steps toward reconciliation.

TRC Call to Action #62 (Education)

We call upon the federal, provincial, and territorial governments, in consultation and collaboration with Survivors, Aboriginal peoples, and educators, to make age-appropriate curriculum on residential schools, Treaties, and Aboriginal peoples' historical and contemporary contributions to Canada a mandatory requirement for K–12 students across the country.
